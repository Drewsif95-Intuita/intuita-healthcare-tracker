The situation

A high-growth full-fibre business sitting on a data architecture that couldn't keep up

The client is one of the UK's largest full-fibre broadband providers, serving residential and business customers in multi-dwelling units and commercial developments. The business was in a period of rapid network expansion and customer growth, with significant pressure on ARPU, retention and operating cost — and a growing strategic requirement to move toward AI-enabled customer operations.

The data capability underneath those ambitions wasn't going to get them there. The picture across the business was familiar but acute:

  • No single, trusted view of customer or premise. Data was fragmented across CRM, billing, OSS and ServiceNow, with no governed model joining them.
  • Logic scattered across tools. Transformations and definitions lived in the warehouse and in the visualisation layer, leading to duplication, inconsistency, and low confidence in the numbers.
  • UPRN fragility. The Unique Property Reference Number was being used as a join key, which caused reconciliation issues, manual rework, and historical data loss whenever a premise was reassigned.
  • Insight cycles measured in weeks. Pricing, operational and executive decisions were waiting on data that should have been near-real-time.
  • No viable foundation for AI. Neither the data trust nor the historical depth needed to train models existed.

The Head of Data and Digital sponsored the programme with a clear remit: mature the organisation's data capability, enable a joined-up customer view across acquisition, service and billing, and lay the foundations for AI-enabled products in customer operations.

The decision

Build for capability, not for category

The conventional path for a business at this scale is to buy into a major cloud data platform vendor and let the licensing economics define the architecture. The team chose differently.

Together with the client, we made the call to build the Data Hub on a deliberately low-cost, high-capability stack — Iceberg tables on S3, dbt for transformation, open-source orchestration. The decision was not driven by ideology. It was driven by the math: the same capability at a fraction of the licence cost meant more budget left for the AI products that the platform was being built to enable.

"The platform's job wasn't to be impressive. It was to make the AI products on top of it possible."

Four principles shaped the build:

  • Source-to-platform in 10 minutes, incrementally. Real-time enough for any operational decision the business actually needed to make. The architecture was designed around incremental builds into S3 and Iceberg, not heavy overnight batches.
  • One governed domain model. Customer, product, location, network, billing, workforce — modelled once, governed centrally, consumed everywhere. Bespoke extracts replaced with owned datasets.
  • Operating model as a deliverable. Data Product, BI & Research, Engineering, Ops, Governance and AI Ops — all stood up as defined functions with patterns, standards and tooling. The platform would be operated by the client, not just used by them.
  • UPRN as a problem to solve, not work around. A dedicated UPRN base became one of the foundational data products — the joining problem turned into a governed asset.

What this enabled

From "we have data" to "we ship AI"

The two non-negotiable preconditions for AI in a customer operations context are data quality and historical depth. Most organisations have neither when they start trying to build AI products. The Data Hub's job was to establish both.

With those preconditions met, the AI products that came next stopped being experimental and started being operational:

  • Agent Assist. Real-time, in-flow guidance for contact centre agents — drawing on the full customer, premise and product context that previously took agents minutes to assemble manually.
  • Customer operations AI. Models trained on years of governed, reconciled history — not the months of patchy data most platforms would have given them.
  • Propensity, scoring, retention. Run continuously, fed directly into Optimove and outbound channels. From periodic batch to always-on.

And critically: each of these AI products sits on the same Data Hub foundation. New use cases don't require new pipelines, new reconciliation, new arguments about which dataset is correct. They plug in.

What we'd take from this

Three principles for building toward AI, not just toward dashboards

1. AI doesn't fail because of the model. It fails because of the data underneath.

The two conditions for AI in customer operations are quality and history. Most organisations don't have either when they start. Treat the foundation as the deliverable, not the prerequisite.

2. The cheapest stack that meets the requirement is the right stack.

Open-source storage and transformation tooling will, for most organisations, deliver everything a major cloud data platform does at a fraction of the licence cost. The savings fund the AI products the platform is meant to enable. That's a real trade, not an ideological one.

3. Build the operating model in parallel, not afterwards.

A platform without a governance function, an AI Ops function and a data product function isn't a platform — it's just a database that happens to have a logo on it. We delivered the operating model alongside the technology, and the client now operates the platform without us in the room.
