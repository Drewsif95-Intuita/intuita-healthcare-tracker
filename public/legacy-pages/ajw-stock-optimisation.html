The situation

A 90-year-old aviation services business running its inventory on spreadsheets

The client is a leading independent provider of aviation component services and supply chain solutions to over 1,000 airlines worldwide. Headquartered in the UK with global hubs across four continents, the business manages over 500,000 components across Airbus and Boeing fleets — and has built its reputation on global reach, operational excellence and cost-effective solutions.

Underneath that operational excellence sat a planning model that had aged badly. Inventory decisions — what to buy, what to repair, what stock to hold — were being made on:

  • Spreadsheet-driven forecasting with little ability to test alternatives or trade-off strategies
  • Static rules and gut feel, with no way to explore the impact of repair-vs-purchase or stock-reduction decisions before committing
  • One-size-fits-all assumptions on lead time, despite enormous variability between suppliers and part types — a major source of stock-out and over-order risk on critical parts
  • Inconsistent valuation data, with purchase and sales prices not always recorded, and quote-to-transaction ambiguity in the source records

In aviation, the cost of getting these decisions wrong is asymmetric. Stocking too little can ground an aircraft. Stocking too much locks up working capital that could be deployed elsewhere. The business needed a fundamentally better way to make these calls.

The approach

A modular simulation engine, built to test thousands of futures

Rather than buy a packaged inventory optimisation product, the team built a bespoke probabilistic forecasting and simulation engine designed to be extended — a foundation, not a finished tool.

"For less than £3,000 in additional infrastructure, the business gained the ability to test 1,000 scenarios per part, across 8,500 high-priority parts."

The approach combined four ideas, deliberately:

  • Part classification first. Every part was statistically profiled into one of five demand types — Smooth, Intermittent, Lumpy, Erratic, or Insufficient Data. This is the move most spreadsheet-based systems skip; it turned out to be the move that made everything else work.
  • ML model selection per class. Each demand class got the model best suited to its pattern complexity and data richness. No more "one forecasting method for everything."
  • Scenario simulation, not point forecasts. Every part was run through 1,000 simulated conditions to identify the optimal trade-off between purchase, repair and stock reduction.
  • Demand and lead time uncertainty modelled together. Critical for aviation parts, where supplier lead time variability is often the single biggest source of stock-out risk.

Decisions answered

The questions a planner can now actually ask

The shift from spreadsheets to a simulation engine isn't a tooling upgrade — it's a change in what's askable. Decisions that previously required offline analysis (or were left to gut feel) became standing questions the planner could pose at any time.

Question 01

Which scenario gives the best ROI while still meeting demand?

Per part. Across thousands of parts. Updated continuously as data refreshes — not a quarterly analyst exercise.

Question 02

What's the budget required for the top 500 parts by ROI?

A direct, defensible number for procurement planning — replacing the rolled-up estimates that had previously gone to the board.

Question 03

How does a cost-reduction strategy affect revenue and penalties?

Modelled, not assumed. The trade-offs are visible before they're committed to — including for parts that were previously invisible to the optimisation process.

Question 04

What's the cumulative benefit of adjusting the purchase-vs-repair mix?

Tested across thousands of simulated futures, ranked by value. The repair-versus-purchase debate becomes a data question, not an opinion question.

Where else this applies

The pattern travels — wherever inventory, demand and uncertainty meet

The probabilistic simulation framework was built for aviation parts but the methodology generalises cleanly. Wherever a business is making stock-and-supply decisions under demand and lead-time uncertainty, the same approach delivers value.

Predicting demand more accurately to ensure the right stock is in the right place at the right time. Reducing end-of-season markdowns by avoiding overstock at the point of buy.

Modelling supplier lead time variability against production schedules — protecting throughput without locking working capital into safety stock.

Product launch and seasonality optimisation

Optimised stock levels reduce obsolescence and end-of-life write-offs. Smaller, more frequent production runs reduce the environmental and financial cost of the supply chain.

What we'd take from this

Three principles for ML that makes operational decisions, not predictions

1. Classify first. Model second.

The single biggest determinant of forecast accuracy in inventory ML is matching the model to the demand pattern. Most off-the-shelf forecasting tools skip this step. A statistical classifier — five buckets, applied at the start — turns a difficult problem into five tractable ones.

2. Simulate, don't predict.

A point forecast tells a planner what's most likely. A scenario simulation tells them the trade-offs across thousands of plausible futures. Operational decisions are about trade-offs, not probabilities. The output that matters is the ranked decision, not the predicted number.

3. The cheapest infrastructure that holds the model is the right infrastructure.

For under £3,000 in incremental infrastructure, the simulation engine ran 1,000 scenarios per part across 8,500 priority parts. Most ML projects spend more on infrastructure than they need to. Modular, cloud-native architectures keep the cost-to-value ratio honest.
