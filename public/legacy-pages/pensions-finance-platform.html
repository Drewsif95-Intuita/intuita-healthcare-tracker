The situation

A 20-year Oracle estate that had become the bottleneck it was meant to solve

The client is a major UK pensions and savings provider, administering tens of billions of pounds in assets across workplace pensions, individual savings and retirement income products. A growth strategy built on book-of-business acquisitions had collided with a finance estate that couldn't absorb new data fast enough — and every onboarding programme was exposing the same brittle dependencies.

The platform that sat at the centre of it all was an Oracle E-Business Suite estate that had accreted more than two decades of customisation. The technology problems were the obvious ones:

  • Rigid change management. Accounting rule modifications required expensive regression testing because of the interwoven application logic. Change requests took three to six months to deliver — and each delay was absorbed back into the business as a manual workaround.
  • Manual month-end close. Month-end close was running substantially longer than industry benchmark, with finance teams maintaining a sprawl of spreadsheets, email approvals and re-keyed adjustments to get data into the GL.
  • Vendor-controlled roadmap. Oracle dictated upgrade timing and functionality. The business adapted to Oracle's roadmap rather than the other way round — and customisation without vendor support had become economically unviable.
  • Limited audit trail. Lineage gaps and manual reconciliations made regulatory and audit responses costly to produce and slow to defend.
  • Increasing licensing costs with Oracle Cloud options materially more expensive than the on-premises estate they were intended to replace — and misaligned with the rest of the organisation's IT architecture strategy.

The less obvious problem — and the one that would shape the engagement most — was cultural. The finance function had ten-plus years of deep Oracle EBS workflow knowledge, real concerns about cloud security for financial data, and a culture of vendor dependency: a default expectation that Oracle, or another vendor, would handle the technical decisions. Any new platform would only land if the finance team came with it.

The decision

A componentised, cloud-native rebuild — with finance in the cockpit

The conventional path was to take the Oracle Cloud upgrade and absorb the licence cost. Together with the client, we made a different call. Rather than lift-and-shift the legacy estate or accept the Oracle Cloud trajectory, we re-architected the financial accounting platform from first principles — built natively on Azure and Databricks, with a parametrised model that turns ingestion, transformation and orchestration into reusable components.

"The technology rebuild was the visible deliverable. The cultural rebuild — finance owning the platform — was the one that determined whether any of it lasted."

Five principles shaped the build:

  • Componentised, not monolithic. Every capability — ingest, transform, post, observe — became its own component with a clear contract. New data sources slot into the same patterns rather than spawning new code.
  • Parametrised by default. Pipelines are configured, not hand-coded. Adding a new source or rule changes a config, not a codebase — making delivery faster, testable, and consistent. Critical for absorbing acquisitions at the cadence the business actually wanted.
  • Governed end-to-end. Collibra owns the rules and definitions; Databricks enforces them inline. Lineage and controls are part of the platform, not bolted on for audit cycles.
  • Finance in the driver's seat. Power BI for full observability, Power Apps for self-service rerun and exception handling — finance owns the day-to-day, IT focuses on the platform itself.
  • Hybrid resourcing, knowledge transfer as a deliverable. The delivery team was organised as a single hybrid squad with shared ownership across FSP and the client — designed from day one to leave the platform in the client's hands at the end.

Platform building blocks

The components the finance backbone is built on

⚡

Power Apps

Finance-owned self-service. Rerun failed pipelines. Manage exceptions. Approve postings. Raise queries. The interface that gives finance day-to-day control.

What this enabled

From "raise a ticket and wait" to "rerun it yourself"

Each of the six capabilities below replaces a chronic friction point in the legacy operating model. None of them are individually glamorous; together they redefine what the finance function can do without intervention from IT.

⏱

Faster month-end close

Pre-aggregated, rule-validated postings flow automatically into GL Fusion. Spreadsheet reconciliation and manual journal keying are no longer on the critical path.

◧

Acquisition onboarding by config

New books of business connect to the platform via configuration rather than custom code. The integration cycle drops from months to weeks — and the per-acquisition cost drops with it.

⊞

Audit-ready by default

End-to-end source-to-posting lineage in Databricks; rule provenance evidenced in Collibra. Audit and regulatory responses become a query, not a project.

↻

Self-service rerun & exceptions

When pipelines fail or exceptions appear, finance investigates and triages directly in Power Apps. IT involvement is for platform changes, not for operating the platform.

⊡

Real-time operational reporting

Power BI dashboards on pipeline health, control outcomes and exception status — replacing the after-the-fact reporting that left the team reacting rather than steering.

⌬

Modern integration surface

API-first design with reverse feeds from the GL and a Unity Catalog model for sharing governed assets across domains. The platform now integrates outward instead of resisting integration.

Cultural transformation

A finance team that didn't just adopt the platform — they came to own it

The thing that surprises most observers of this engagement is not the architecture diagram. It's where the finance team ended up.

The team arrived with ten-plus years of EBS workflow knowledge, real concerns about cloud security for financial data, and a default expectation that vendors handled the technical decisions. Within the engagement, those concerns didn't go away by being dismissed — they went away by being designed for. Quick wins through Power Automate and Power BI prototypes were shipped early. Cloud expertise was transferred inside the squad, not delivered to it. Squad ownership was deliberately fluid between FSP and client members — the line between "us" and "them" was kept thin on purpose.

What that produced, eighteen months in:

  • Finance teams now own and manage the product. Day-to-day operations, exception handling and minor change are all in their hands.
  • In-house Azure expertise has developed across the team. The platform is no longer a black box maintained by someone else.
  • The team moved from resistance to advocacy. The people who arrived sceptical are now the ones running new features across the product and championing the operating model to adjacent functions.
  • Agile is now native to how finance plans and ships. Continuous improvement is the operating cadence rather than a quarterly initiative.

"The platform delivered the technical capability. The squad model delivered the team that now operates it. Neither would have worked alone."

What we'd take from this

Three principles for retiring legacy estates in regulated environments

1. Componentise. Then parametrise. Then ship.

The reason this platform absorbs new acquisitions in weeks rather than months is not that it's faster than the Oracle estate it replaced. It's that adding a source is a configuration change, not a code change. Componentised, parametrised, contract-driven design is the technical decision that compounds for years after go-live.

2. Build governance into the platform, not next to it.

Audit and lineage as a retrofit is a project. Audit and lineage as part of the pipeline is a property. In a regulated environment, the difference between those two stances determines whether the platform is sustainable. Collibra defining the rules, Databricks enforcing them inline — same idea, applied consistently across every domain.

3. The team is the deliverable, not just the platform.

Cloud migrations that leave the client dependent on the consultancy haven't migrated anything — they've moved the dependency. The hybrid squad model, with shared ownership and knowledge transfer as a daily practice rather than a sign-off line item, is what turned this platform from a vendor handover into a finance-owned product. It's also the part of the engagement most clients don't ask for explicitly. They should.
