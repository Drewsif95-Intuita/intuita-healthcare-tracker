The situation

A national rollout running on spreadsheets, with no common definitions

The client is one of the UK's largest wholesale full-fibre infrastructure providers, building and operating a national network across [X]+ towns and cities. The business is run on a small set of high-stakes operational and quality metrics — including Right First Time (RFT) for build quality and Ready For Service (RFS) for commercial state — alongside the financial reporting that tracks how the rollout translates into wholesale revenue.

When the engagement started, the data capability underneath all of that was fragmented:

  • No centralised platform. Reporting was distributed across individual Salesforce reports, scattered Excel workbooks, and a sprawl of manual data entities that had grown up to fill gaps in the strategic systems.
  • Inconsistent KPI definitions. RFT, RFS and other core metrics could be calculated different ways by different teams. The same metric name meant different things depending on who you asked — and time was being lost negotiating which number was correct rather than acting on it.
  • Finance reporting run by hand. The team was assembling reports manually across spreadsheets each cycle. Slow, error-prone, and impossible to evidence cleanly.
  • No historical record of how data was changing. Forecasts, plans and other time-varying figures were overwritten with every update. There was no way to look back and ask "what did we think six months ago, and how has that shifted?" — the kind of question the business needed to answer constantly.
  • Executive reporting derived, not designed. Board reports were assembled by pulling and reconciling data from multiple teams, after the fact. Limited transparency on how the headline numbers were composed.
  • Manual entities masking system gaps. The reliance on manual data entities had become a strategic problem — they were both papering over gaps in the source systems and creating their own data quality issues.

The brief was unambiguous: build the data capability the business needed end-to-end. Not a platform, then a reporting layer, then enablement — all three, in sequence, as a single programme.

The decision

Standardise the KPIs. Then build everything else on them.

The temptation in a programme of this shape is to take each reporting requirement as it arrives and build it. A dashboard for the build team. A dashboard for finance. A dashboard for the exec. Pile them up. Hope the layer underneath holds.

Together with the client, we made a different call. Before any major dashboard was built, the core KPIs were refined and standardised — one governed definition per metric, agreed across the business, encoded into the warehouse. The data foundation was then designed in line with capability — what the business needs to be able to do across many cycles of asking — rather than designed against the user story currently in front of us.

"Data will be designed in line with capability — as opposed to each individual user story."

Six principles shaped the build:

  • KPI definitions, standardised and governed. RFT, RFS, build progress, financial measures — refined with the business, agreed once, encoded in the warehouse. The same metric now means the same thing whether it appears on a board dashboard or in an analyst's exploration.
  • Lake first, warehouse second. A landing layer in S3 took ingestion and raw retention out of the warehouse's job description. Snowflake could then be optimised for the modelled, governed reporting layer it was actually meant to serve.
  • Conformed dimensions and facts as a foundation. A single dimensional spine — premise, build phase, region, time — that every downstream report joins to, by construction.
  • Snapshot history for time-varying data. Forecasts, plans and other figures that change over time are snapshotted into the warehouse so the business can compare what was forecast six months ago to what's happening now — and watch how forecasts shift over time rather than only ever seeing the latest one.
  • Tableau as the consumption standard. One BI surface, one set of design standards, one governance model — board-level dashboards and operational analyst views built on the same underlying datasets, with consistency visible to anyone moving between them.
  • Training and standards alongside delivery. Adoption is what makes the platform real. Onboarding sessions, BI design standards, and dashboard development support were delivered alongside the engineering — not bolted on at the end.

A capability the business didn't have before

Snapshotting: how forecasts move over time, not just where they sit today

One of the most useful capabilities the warehouse unlocked wasn't visible in any single dashboard. It was the ability to ask how things have changed.

Forecasts, plans and other time-varying figures are useful in the moment, but most reporting environments overwrite them every cycle. Whatever the forecast was last month is gone the moment this month's forecast lands. The business loses the ability to see how its own view of the future has shifted — and to learn from those shifts.

We built snapshot tables into the warehouse for the figures that mattered most. Forecasts, plans, build estimates, financial projections — captured at each refresh cycle and retained, with the historical record available alongside the latest state.

That turned a fixed-in-time dashboard culture into something more honest. What did we think six months ago? How has our forecast shifted, and against actuals? Where are we consistently optimistic, and where are we conservative? Questions the business hadn't been able to answer before, because the data needed to answer them had been overwritten every cycle.

That capability sits inside the same governed warehouse as the headline reporting, on the same conformed dimensions, with the same KPI definitions — so the historical view and the current view are always comparable.

What we'd take from this

Three principles for end-to-end transformation from zero

1. Standardise the definitions before you standardise the dashboards.

The dashboards are the visible part; the definitions are the part that determines whether they ever agree with each other. Refining the core KPIs with the business — one governed definition per metric — and encoding them into the warehouse before the main build costs time on the first dashboard and a fraction as much on every dashboard after. Design for capability, not for the user story currently in front of you.

2. Lake and warehouse are different jobs. Build them as different jobs.

S3 is for keeping data: cheap, permissive, schema-on-read, retain everything. Snowflake is for serving data: governed, modelled, performant, expensive enough that you don't put raw ingestion through it. Compressing the two into one layer is a common shortcut that pays for itself negatively. Two layers, two purposes, clear boundary between them.

3. Adoption is a deliverable, not a phase.

Build the platform and the dashboards. Then hold a "training week" at the end. That's the playbook that produces an adopted platform on paper and an unused one in practice. Training, BI design standards, and dashboard development support belong alongside the engineering — so people are using the platform while it's still being built. That's how it ends up running the business rather than sitting next to the business.
