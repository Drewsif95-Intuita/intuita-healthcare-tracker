The situation

£100m of community investment, and a need to defend every pound of it

The client is a major UK housing association setting up a Community Foundation to deliver more than £100m of investment across communities over the next decade. Significant work had already been done internally to improve the underlying data and to build foundational analytical assets — including an Asset Grading model — before this engagement began.

What the organisation now needed was a step change: a scoring model bringing together internal and external data sources to produce a defensible, explainable, comparable community indicator. One that could:

  • Justify the investment programme. Funding decisions of this scale need objective, defensible evidence — not anecdotal need.
  • Be explainable to non-technical stakeholders. Trustees, communities, partner organisations and regulators all need to understand why a community received the score it did.
  • Operate at multiple granularities. National comparability at Local Authority level was the headline; MSOA and LSOA decomposition was needed for actual targeting.
  • Be portable. The model was designed from the start with the option of being shared with other housing associations across the country — turning a single organisation's analytical investment into sector infrastructure.
The approach

Build the model in stages, prove it before scaling it

The approach was deliberately conservative for a model of this consequence: a 6-week proof of concept, followed by an 18-week full build. The POC existed not to test whether a model was possible, but to validate the shape of the model — what subcomponents to use, what data was reliable, what weighting scheme would produce defensible scores.

"A composite indicator is only as defensible as its weights. We treated weighting as a research question, not a configuration option."

Four design choices shaped the build:

  • Five sub-component pillars. Health, Crime, Education, Financial Wellbeing, and Amenities — chosen for both data availability and demonstrable causal relevance to community wellbeing.
  • Internal and external data combined. Internal SNG data on properties and tenants, layered with national-level public data — Census, health, mobility, education, crime — to provide a view that's specific where it can be and comparable where it needs to be.
  • Hierarchical decomposition. Each pillar breaks down into sub-component groups, then individual indicators. The score at the top is always traceable back to the underlying data.
  • National scaling on three boundaries. Each component is scaled against national, county, and Local Authority averages — so a community's score reflects its position both locally and across the whole country.

Decisions answered

The questions a trustee, an analyst and a community partner can now actually ask

The score is not the deliverable. The deliverable is the ability to ask better questions about where investment goes — and to defend the answers when challenged.

Question 01

Which communities most need the next phase of investment?

Ranked nationally and within county, with the score decomposable so the case for each can be made on its specific drivers.

Question 02

Why does this community score the way it does?

Not a black-box answer. The model exposes the contribution of each pillar, each sub-group, and each indicator — to anyone who needs to see it.

Question 03

How does this community compare nationally?

Scaled against national, county and Local Authority averages — the score answers "compared to what?" before anyone asks.

Question 04

Where exactly should investment land within the community?

MSOA and LSOA decomposition turns Local Authority-level signals into block-level targeting decisions.

What we'd take from this

Three principles for composite indicator models that have to be defended

1. Explainability is the deliverable, not a feature.

For a composite score that drives investment of this consequence, the score itself is the smallest part of the value. The ability to decompose, defend and discuss the score with non-technical stakeholders is what determines whether the model gets used.

2. The weights are a research question.

Composite indicators are only as defensible as their weighting scheme. Treating weighting as a configuration choice is the move that turns a model into a black box. Treating it as a research question — driven by foundational analysis and stakeholder input — is the move that earns trust.

3. Build for sharing if you can.

Models built for one organisation tend to die with that organisation. Models built with sector-portability in mind, on common public datasets, become infrastructure that outlives the original engagement. The economics are different. So is the long-term impact.
