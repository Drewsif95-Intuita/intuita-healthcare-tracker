The situation

A complex business sitting on fragmented data

The client is one of the UK's largest telecommunications providers, delivering mobile, broadband, home phone and TV services to millions of customers. The business was actively integrating its fixed-line and mobile portfolios — historically managed on entirely separate platforms — while operating in a market where customer acquisition, cross-sell and churn reduction were the defining commercial priorities.

Internally, the picture was harder. Data warehouses, source systems and reporting teams had grown up independently, each solving local problems without a shared backbone. The consequences showed up in three places that mattered:

  • Data science was stuck in preparation. Teams spent around 80% of their time wrangling data and 20% building models. Churn propensity — the most commercially important model in the business — ran monthly and retrospectively. By the time the model identified a customer at risk, the customer had already left.
  • Reporting had no single version of the truth. Different teams produced reports independently, often with subtly different definitions of the same KPI. Trust in the numbers was the casualty.
  • The customer view was split in two. Mobile and fixed-line data lived in separate platforms with no consolidated view of customers who held both products — exactly the customers the business most wanted to understand.

The Director of Data and Analytics, reporting to the CMO, sponsored the programme with a clear remit: advance the organisation's data maturity, enable faster and more trusted insight across all product lines, and do it without waiting for the multi-year enterprise transformation already on the long-term roadmap.

The decision

Build alongside, not instead of

The conventional answer to a problem this size is a full enterprise data consolidation programme. Big budget, big timeline, central IT-owned, three-to-five years to value.

We didn't take that path.

Together with the client, we made the call to build OneView as a net-new analytics platform alongside the existing enterprise warehouses, accepting deliberate storage duplication in exchange for moving in months rather than years.

"In data, the right architecture is rarely the architecturally pure one. It's the one that lets the business move now."

Four principles shaped the build from day one:

  • Ingest from the warehouses, not the sources. Source systems were only used where the central warehouses didn't already hold the data. This decision alone removed months of integration work.
  • Build alongside, not instead of. OneView was designed to coexist with the long-term enterprise transformation, not block it. It aligned to the eventual target architecture so the work would carry forward.
  • Joint delivery team. A blended team brought our data and modelling expertise together with the client's SME knowledge and direct understanding of what reporting and data science teams actually needed.
  • Feed the data-hungry teams first. Data science was the priority consumer. Every design choice — flat tables, model-ready schemas, fast query performance — was optimised for the people who would generate the most commercial value first.
The architecture

A layered platform with clear separation of concerns

Platform products

The things that made the platform trustworthy

OneView shipped with three internal products that turned the platform from a piece of infrastructure into something its users could rely on. Without these, self-serve analytics doesn't happen. With them, the platform scales.

✓

Data quality monitoring

Daily automated checks on completeness and consistency across every dataset, with alerts when thresholds were breached. Every KPI shipped with a known confidence level.

◆

Pipeline & layer availability

Operational visibility into whether daily data loads completed successfully and on time. Stale data could no longer flow silently into dashboards or models.

⊞

Data catalogue & business wiki

Searchable documentation down to column level — metric definitions, lineage, SME-recorded sessions. Self-serve analytics became possible at scale.

Business products

What the platform unlocked commercially

With the foundation in place, OneView became a launchpad for the products the business actually monetised. Several of these alone funded the platform many times over within the first year.

📊

Propensity models

Churn, cross-sell and upsell propensity consuming OneView's L2 layer directly. Cadence shifted from monthly retrospective to weekly operational.

⊕

Marketing effectiveness & attribution

Cross-channel and third-party performance measurement. For every pound spent — exactly where, and exactly how it returned.

↗

Outbound dialler optimisation

Call campaign tuning that drove higher conversion, longer-tenure conversions, and far less wasted agent time across the contact centre.

◇

Customer engagement insight

Feature-level usage analysis that fed directly back into product development priorities — building the things customers actually used.

▶

TV recommendation engine

Lookalike-profile and viewing-history-driven recommendations that deepened product engagement and reduced TV churn.

⊡

Regulatory & consolidated reporting

Replacing fragmented, contradictory team-level reports with a single trusted source covering customer, product, billing and contact centre.

What we'd take from this

Three lessons we apply to almost every platform engagement

1. The right architecture is the one the business can use now.

Pure enterprise consolidation programmes are right in theory and wrong in practice when the business needs answers in months. Building alongside, with a clear path to eventual convergence, beats building instead of.

2. Data scientists don't want clever — they want flat, wide and ready.

Every layer of OneView's L2 was designed for direct model consumption with zero further wrangling. The cadence shift from monthly to weekly model refresh wasn't a technology achievement — it was a design achievement. The data scientists got their time back.

3. Documentation is a product, not a deliverable.

The wikis, catalogues and trust scores were treated as first-class platform products with their own roadmap. Without them, self-serve analytics doesn't happen — people just keep asking the data team. With them, the platform scales beyond its original sponsors.
