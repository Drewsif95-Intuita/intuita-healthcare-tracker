Week 1 — Virgin Media OneView · Post 1 (Tease)

A leading UK telco was sitting on a multi-year data transformation roadmap.

Their data scientists were spending 80% of their time preparing data, not building models. Churn propensity — their most commercially important model — was running monthly. By the time it flagged a customer, the customer had already left.

The “right” answer was a full enterprise consolidation. Three years, eight figures, central IT.

We didn’t do that.

We built a net-new analytics platform alongside the existing warehouses. Accepted storage duplication. Got months instead of years.

Within the first year:

→ Churn propensity moved from monthly to weekly. Models could iterate daily. → 20× query performance versus the central platforms. → Platform paid for itself many times over through propensity-driven revenue.

Week 2 — Virgin Media OneView · Post 2 (Deliver)

80% of a data scientist’s time spent on data prep is not a productivity problem.

It’s a platform problem.

I see this pattern across data teams in financial services, NHS, telecoms, utilities — brilliant data scientists, expensive to hire, building careful models on data they spent four days cleaning before they could touch it. Then doing it again the next week.

The fix isn’t more headcount or better tools. It’s the layer underneath them.

When we delivered a recent telco data platform engagement, the single design decision that changed everything was building a layer specifically for data science consumption — wide, flat, model-ready tables that plugged directly into models with zero further wrangling.

The cadence shift surprised the business: their churn propensity model moved from monthly retrospective to weekly operational. Daily iteration became possible for the first time.

The data scientists didn’t get better. They got their time back.

If your data team is “underperforming,” check what they’re actually doing day-to-day before you change anything else.

Week 3 — Virgin Media OneView · Post 3 (Reflect)

Three things I’ve learned delivering data platforms that I think apply to almost any large data programme:

1. The right architecture is the one the business can use now.

Pure enterprise consolidation programmes are right in theory and wrong in practice when the business needs answers in months. Building alongside, with a clear path to eventual convergence, beats building instead of.

2. Data scientists don’t want clever — they want flat, wide, and ready.

The cadence shift from monthly to weekly model refresh isn’t a technology achievement. It’s a design achievement. Optimise the layer underneath the data scientists; everything else follows.

3. Documentation is a product, not a deliverable.

Every dataset on a recent telco platform we delivered shipped with a daily data quality score attached. “My source data is 97% complete” is a sentence a data scientist can act on. “The data is fine” is not.

The thing that ties all three together: the platforms that scale aren’t the technically impressive ones. They’re the ones the people downstream actually want to use.

Week 4 — HyperOptic DataHub · Post 1 (Tease)

Most data platforms don’t fail because of the technology.

They fail because the AI products that were supposed to sit on top of them never get built — or get built on data the business doesn’t trust.

A UK full-fibre provider came to us with that exact problem. Customer data scattered across CRM, billing, OSS and ServiceNow. Insight cycles measured in weeks. No viable foundation for the AI customer operations products their leadership wanted to ship.

We built a Data Hub on a deliberately low-cost, high-capability stack. Iceberg on S3. Open-source orchestration. Source-to-platform refresh in 10 minutes.

Once the foundation was in place, the AI products that came next stopped being experimental and started being operational. Agent Assist. Customer Ops AI. Propensity, scoring, retention — all running on the same governed foundation.

The platform’s job wasn’t to be impressive. It was to make the AI products on top of it possible.

Week 5 — HyperOptic DataHub · Post 2 (Deliver)

The cheapest stack that meets the requirement is the right stack.

Most data platform decisions get made the wrong way round. You start with a vendor — Snowflake, Databricks, Microsoft Fabric — and let the licensing economics define the architecture. Then you spend 30%+ of your platform budget on licences before any business value lands.

On a recent telco data platform we delivered, the team made the opposite call. Iceberg tables on S3. dbt for transformation. Open-source orchestration.

Same capability. Fraction of the licence cost.

The savings funded the AI products the platform was being built to enable in the first place. Agent Assist for the contact centre. Customer Ops AI. Propensity models running continuously, fed directly into outbound channels.

This isn’t an ideological argument. It’s a math one. Most platforms over-spend on licences because that’s the conservative-looking choice. It isn’t — not when it forecloses the AI roadmap on the other side.

Week 6 — HyperOptic DataHub · Post 3 (Reflect)

AI doesn’t fail because of the model.

It fails because of the data underneath.

Two non-negotiable conditions exist for AI in any customer operations context: data quality and historical depth. Most organisations have neither when they start trying to ship AI products. They go straight to model selection, then spend the next two years explaining to the business why it isn’t working.

Treat the data foundation as the deliverable, not the prerequisite.

When we delivered a recent telco data platform, every dataset went out the door with a daily quality score attached. “My source data is 97% complete” is a sentence a data scientist can act on. “The data is fine” is not.

Once both conditions were met — quality and history — the AI products on top stopped being PowerPoint and started being production. Agent Assist live in the contact centre. Customer Ops AI on years of governed history rather than months of patchy data.

The platforms that scale to AI aren’t the technically impressive ones. They’re the ones the people downstream actually trust.

A recent retailer we worked with had been sitting on idle Snowflake for months when we arrived. Seven months later, customer lifetime value, product profitability and the real cost of returns were all measurable for the first time.

Week 9 — Oh Polly Transformation · Post 3 (Reflect)

Three things that high-growth retailers underestimate when building data capability:

1. The unglamorous layers do the work.

Common data models. Data quality monitoring. Documentation. None of it lands you a board mention. All of it determines whether the analytics on top actually works. Build up the maturity pyramid in sequence. The exciting layers don’t survive without the boring ones underneath.

2. Operations data deserves its own layer.

Warehouse operations need feedback loops measured in minutes, not hours. Trying to serve operational and reporting use cases from the same refresh cadence creates platforms that are bad at both. A separate operational layer in 15-minute batches kept the reporting platform clean — and gave operations the real-time visibility they actually need.

3. Tools don’t make a capability. People do.

The two most common things I see in retail data: idle Snowflake, and dashboards no one trusts. Buying the platforms is the easy part. Building the capability to operate them, and the literacy in the business to consume them, is what determines whether the investment lands.

Most retailers learn at least one of these the expensive way.

Week 10 — AJW Stock Optimisation · Post 1 (Tease)

A 90-year-old aviation services business was running its inventory on spreadsheets.

Decisions on what to buy, what to repair, what stock to hold — across 500,000+ components — were being made on static rules and gut feel.

In aviation, the cost of getting these decisions wrong is asymmetric. Stock too little, you ground an aircraft. Stock too much, you lock up working capital that should be deployed elsewhere.

For under £3,000 in incremental infrastructure, we built them a probabilistic forecasting and simulation engine. Each part now runs through 1,000 simulated conditions to identify the optimal trade-off between purchase, repair and stock reduction.

The shift wasn’t the technology. It was the question that became askable:

“Which scenario gives the best ROI while still meeting demand?”

A planner can now ask that, per part, across thousands of parts, continuously. Previously it was a quarterly analyst exercise — if it happened at all.

Week 11 — AJW Stock Optimisation · Post 2 (Reflect)

A point forecast tells a planner what’s most likely to happen.

A scenario simulation tells them the trade-offs across thousands of plausible futures.

Operational decisions are about trade-offs, not probabilities. The output that matters isn’t the predicted number — it’s the ranked decision.

Most off-the-shelf forecasting tools skip the step that makes this work: classifying the data before modelling it. We’ve used a five-bucket statistical classifier (Smooth, Intermittent, Lumpy, Erratic, Insufficient Data) on a recent aviation engagement, with a tailored ML model selected per class. It turns a difficult problem into five tractable ones.

The pattern travels well beyond aviation. Anywhere stock-and-supply decisions need to be made under demand and lead-time uncertainty — retail, manufacturing, e-commerce, even ESG-driven supply chain optimisation — the same approach delivers value.

Three things I’d take from this into any inventory ML programme:

→ Classify first. Model second. The biggest gains come from matching the model to the demand pattern. → Simulate, don’t predict. A ranked decision beats an accurate forecast every time when the buyer is a planner. → The cheapest infrastructure that holds the model is the right infrastructure. We ran 1,000 scenarios per part across 8,500 priority parts on under £3,000 of incremental infrastructure.

Week 12 — Sovereign Community Model · Single hero post

A UK housing association is investing more than £100m across communities over the next decade.

Decisions of that scale need objective evidence — not anecdotal need. They need to defend the funding choices to trustees, communities, regulators and partner organisations. They need to point to a defensible reason why this community received funding and that one didn’t.

So we built them a community scoring model.

Five sub-component pillars: Health, Crime, Education, Financial Wellbeing, Amenities. Each decomposes into sub-component groups, then individual indicators traceable back to public data. The composite score for any community can be unpacked all the way down to the source numbers — and challenged at any level.

Three nested granularities: Local Authority for national comparison. MSOA and LSOA for local targeting. The score for a community always answers “compared to what?” before anyone asks.

The model is portable by design. It runs on common public datasets and was built so it could be shared with other housing associations across the country — turning one organisation’s analytical investment into sector infrastructure.

Composite indicators are only as defensible as their weighting scheme. Treating weighting as a configuration choice is the move that turns a model into a black box. Treating it as a research question is the move that earns trust.

Week 13 — Co-Op Managed Service · Single hero post

The hardest part of running a modern data team isn’t the technology.

It’s staffing it.

The skills required to operate a data ecosystem at any meaningful scale span data architecture, engineering, analytics engineering, governance and visualisation. Building a complete in-house team in that mix has been getting harder, not easier — and retaining one while priorities shift is an ongoing tax on time and budget.

A major UK retail co-operative arrived at exactly that decision point. 4,000+ stores, 13 business entities, growing complexity, and an in-house data team they were spending too much leadership attention on staffing.

What they needed was scale-on-demand expertise without the recruiting overhead.

We delivered it as a dual-tier managed service:

→ Tier 1 — continuous operations: pipelines, data quality, monitoring, standard reporting → Tier 2 — specialist on-demand: architecture, senior engineering, analytics engineering, visualisation design → Bolt-ons that flex: capacity added or removed as business needs shift, without re-contracting

The structure was deliberately easy to read. Finance can model the cost. Operations can plan against the capacity. IT can rely on the continuity. Predictability beats cleverness every time in long-term operational relationships.

If you’re spending too much of your data leadership time on staffing, the answer might not be a different recruiter.

Posting mechanics

Roles

  • Drew Chapman (Principal Consultant): owns Posts 1, 4, 7 (the launch posts for each Tier 1 case study), Posts 10 and 12 (AJW lead, Sovereign hero post)
  • Second senior practitioner — a delivery lead from each engagement, ideally: owns Posts 2, 5, 8 (the deliver posts), and Post 13 (Co-Op hero post)
  • Third practitioner / domain expert: owns Posts 3, 6, 9, 11 (the reflect posts)

This rotation means no single person is posting more than once every 2-3 weeks, which is sustainable. It also broadens algorithmic reach across multiple networks.

Cross-amplification

When each post goes live, the other two posters comment on it within the first hour. Substantive comments — pull out a number, ask a question, add a perspective. Three minutes per post; materially better algorithmic performance.

Company page reshare

24+ hours after each personal post, the FSP company page reshares with a one-line framing comment. Don’t reshare same-day — it cannibalises the personal post’s reach.

Measurement

Tag every link with UTM parameters. Review weekly: - Which posts drove traffic to the case study? - Which posts got the most engagement (reactions, comments, reposts)? - Are certain hooks consistently outperforming others?

After Post 6 (HyperOptic week 3), do a proper review and adjust the remaining campaign based on what’s working. Not all 13 posts will land equally — that’s expected. Use the data.

Things to track for each post

  • Impressions (LinkedIn analytics)
  • Reactions / comments / reposts (LinkedIn analytics)
  • Click-throughs to case study (UTM tracking in GA)
  • Time on page after click (GA)
  • Inbound enquiries tagged to source post

Two-week reset post

Around week 7-8 (mid-campaign), consider posting a “reset” piece — not promoting a case study, just a reflection or industry observation. Keeps the audience engaged with you-as-a-person, not just you-as-a-content-machine. Won’t drive case study traffic, but supports the surrounding posts that will.
