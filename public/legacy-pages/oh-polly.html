The situation

A fast-growing retailer whose data capability hadn't kept pace

The client is one of the UK's fastest-growing women's fashion online retailers. Founded a decade ago and grown from an eBay business to a multi-million-pound global operation, the business runs three brands across five websites, employs more than 1,500 people worldwide, and ships to customers across the globe.

The growth had outstripped the data capability underneath it. The picture across the business was classic high-growth retail data debt:

  • No consolidated view of the business. Three brands and five websites meant five different reporting realities. Total business performance was almost impossible to assess without manual consolidation.
  • Reporting in Excel and PowerPoint. Hours of manual processing per cycle. Inconsistent quality. Limited trust in the resulting numbers — and therefore in the decisions made from them.
  • Tools without capability. Fivetran and Snowflake had been bought, but no in-house team existed to use them. The platforms were sitting idle.
  • An ambitious CEO with a data agenda. The leadership team wanted to use data for decision-making, profiling and analytics — but the organisation didn't yet have the foundations to deliver any of it.
  • Limited visualisation experience. Even when reports existed, the audience hadn't been enabled to use them. Education was as much a need as engineering.
The decision

Move the business up the data maturity pyramid, deliberately

The temptation in a high-growth retailer is to start with the most exciting layer of capability — predictive models, customer segmentation, AI — and try to skip the unglamorous foundations. We took the opposite approach.

Together with the client, we mapped the data capability journey as a maturity pyramid: storage and management at the base, through common data models, then data visualisation, then advanced analytics and ML at the top. The decision was to build up the pyramid in sequence, with each layer earning the right to the next.

"The exciting layers don't work without the unglamorous ones underneath. Most retailers learn this the expensive way."

Four principles shaped the build:

  • One common data model across brands. Three brands and five websites would resolve into a single, governed view of the business — finance, merchandising, operations, marketing, all consistent.
  • Operational data in 15-minute batches. Warehouse operations needed near-real-time feedback loops; standing up a separate operational layer kept the rest of the platform unburdened.
  • Education alongside delivery. The team had bought tools they couldn't yet use. Enabling the in-house staff to operate, extend and self-serve was a deliverable, not an afterthought.
  • Build for analytics from day one. The L2 layer was designed for direct model consumption — wide, flat, ready — even though the analytics work would come later in the journey.
The architecture

Foundations that scaled with the business, not against it

What this enabled

The questions the business could finally answer

With the foundations in place, decisions that had been guessed at for years became measurable for the first time. Each one had been a quietly expensive blind spot for a high-growth retailer.

◆

Customer lifetime value

Forwards and backwards in time, by cohort, by brand. The single most important number in retention strategy — finally measurable.

📊

Product & collection profitability

Real margin per product and per collection — not blended averages. Buying decisions made on actual contribution, not assumption.

↩

The real cost of returns

Returns data was previously housed across three platforms, invisible at the business level. Consolidated, the real impact on profitability became visible — and addressable.

⊕

Customer base segmentation

Movement, repeat behaviour, retention patterns. The foundation for every targeted marketing decision the business now makes.

↗

Merchandising triangulation

Pricing, stock availability and sales — joined for the first time. The merchandising team could finally run the function on data, not heuristics.

⚡

Warehouse operations measurement

From stock receipt to 24-hour dispatch, measured in 15-minute increments. Operational performance finally had a number against it.

What we'd take from this

Three lessons for high-growth retailers building data capability

1. Skip the foundations and you'll pay for them later, with interest.

Predictive models and segmentation only work on top of common data models that you can trust. Most growth-stage retailers try to skip the foundations and find themselves rebuilding twice. Build up the pyramid in sequence; it's the cheaper path.

2. Tools don't make a capability. People do.

Snowflake and Fivetran were sitting idle when we arrived. Buying the platforms is the easy bit; building the capability to operate them — and the literacy in the business to consume them — is what determines whether the investment lands.

3. Operations data deserves its own layer.

Warehouse operations need feedback loops measured in minutes, not hours. Standing up a separate operational layer with its own refresh cadence kept the rest of the platform clean — and gave the operations team the real-time visibility their function actually requires.
